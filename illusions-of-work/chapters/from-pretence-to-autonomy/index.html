Chapter 17: From Pretence to Autonomy

The claims management unit ran for fourteen months.

For the first eight, it was the CTO's best argument. Resolution times dropped by thirty-one percent. Customer satisfaction scores for the claims process improved from 3.2 to 4.1. The unit's process definition, versioned and reconciled weekly, had reduced the gap between how claims were supposed to work and how they actually worked to a level the organisation had never achieved. Three other teams had asked how to adopt the model.

Then the CTO was promoted to a group role. His replacement came from the consulting division. She was capable, respected, and had no investment in the autonomous unit model. She did not oppose it. She simply had other priorities.

The CFO, who had been waiting, requested line-item visibility into the unit's spending. The HR business partner reinstated the standard performance review framework. The portfolio governance committee asked the unit lead to present priorities at the quarterly review, “just for alignment.”
