What It Looks Like

Conclusion

This book has described people.

Not systems, not architectures, not frameworks. People. The engineer holding two contradictory models in her head at three in the morning, because the diagram describes one system and production runs another. The architect who presents a reference model he knows is fiction, because the alternative is a conversation nobody wants to have. The business analyst translating between three teams who believe they are describing the same process, knowing they are not, knowing she is the only reason anything connects at all. The operations lead who remembers which applications get stuck and why, because no system tracks it, because nobody ever wrote it down.

These people are not failing. They are coping. They have been coping for years. The illusions of work described in this book are not organisational stupidity. The narratives that replace reality, the fake products, the avoidant architecture, the processes that were never made explicit: none of these are failures of intelligence. They are how intelligent people remain functional inside a structure that will not let them do real work. The vagueness protects them. The fiction gives them something to point to. The translation layers give them a role. Every illusion serves someone's need to remain sane.

Now these people are told to use AI and go faster.
