Capital Mispricing and Structural Risk

Chapter 9: Who Loses When Clarity Wins

A programme director at a European insurer watches the first autonomous unit's quarterly results. Claims resolution time has dropped 31%. Cost per claim has decreased by € 14. The unit deployed forty-three times in its first quarter. His own programme spans six teams, none of which owns a complete process. His last status report was assembled manually by a delivery lead who left last month and has not been replaced.

He reads the unit's results carefully. He is not hostile to the model. He can see that it works. He can also see that his role exists because the organisation has not done what the unit demonstrates is possible. His programme coordinates across teams that do not share ownership. The unit owns its process and coordinates through contracts. His programme produces status reports. The unit produces outcomes.

He begins drafting a proposal for a governance review of the unit's deployment cadence.

The proposal is not cynical. He believes that deployment frequency should be subject to cross-programme visibility. He has spent fifteen years in programme management. The instinct to coordinate is not a choice. It is a professional reflex shaped by a structure that rewarded coordination as a form of value. The structure has changed. The reflex has not. The previous chapters described the structural dysfunction. The chapters that follow describe the correction. This chapter describes what happens in between: the political economy of resistance.
