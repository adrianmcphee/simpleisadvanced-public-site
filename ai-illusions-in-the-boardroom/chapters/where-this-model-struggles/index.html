Structural Correction

Where This Model Struggles

The preceding chapters described a structural model: autonomous units owning complete business processes, interacting through versioned contracts, governed through artefacts rather than narrative. The model is not theoretical. Fragments of it exist wherever ownership is real. It also has boundaries, and naming them is a precondition for applying the model honestly.

Some organisations have already built parts of this structure, usually without naming it as such.

Digital-native firms that grew around a single product often have implicit process ownership. The team that built payments owns payments. The team that built onboarding owns onboarding. Nobody drew a process map or wrote a charter. The boundaries formed because the organisation was small enough that ownership was obvious and the cost of ambiguity was immediate. These firms do not need the model described here. They need to preserve what they already have as they scale, which is a different problem and one that most of them will fail at, because scaling imports the coordination structures this book describes.

Some regulated incumbents have semi-autonomous units, usually in risk management or payments, where regulatory requirements forced a degree of operational independence. A payments team that must demonstrate segregation of duties to the regulator has clearer boundaries than a team that has never been inspected. The autonomy is partial, often accidental, and rarely extends beyond the regulated function. But within that function, the pattern holds: ownership produces clarity, and clarity produces defensibility.
